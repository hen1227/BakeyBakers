In fifth grade, I was intrigued by the idea of having a business and putting my profits back into the business to make it better. I had one successful bake sale on a dirty fold-out table at the corner of our street. My first bake sale was so much fun, so I jokingly started calling it "Bakey Bakers” and I wanted to do it again, but better. I asked my mom about an official stand. I wanted to be more professional. I gave her the money I had earned, and she built me a beautiful stand. I was about $160 in debt. I doubt she was expecting me to pay it back, but I eventually did. I spent many weekends and after school, occasionally with a friend, selling at my stand. I even hired friends to help with marketing and baking goods. Kind of obnoxious now looking back, but I had a Square to collect payment. I didn't like neighbors saying wish they could get something – if only they had available cash! I was in 5th grade, and basically, my lemonade stand accepted credit cards.


I was so happy and proud of myself when I paid my mom back for the stand (Okay, she helped a little with the use of free ingredients.) It felt good to be profitable and out of debt. I even invested in a tiny gumball machine to help raise revenue. I held another couple of stands and made a small profit, but it was around this time my grandpa gave me my first laptop. It was a basic model from Target. I had been experimenting in making websites but nothing more than just hello world and buttons that showed a joke. I talked to a couple of friends who had an actual website on how to create my own. Within a short period, I started my Bakeybakers website. I didn't really know what to have on it, and for about a month, it was just the "BakeyBakers Life" section above. It was this time when I took an afterschool coding class on an app called Processing. I loved it! In between the weekly classes, I created simple things like a ball that bounced around the screen. I quickly got ahead of the course and started making my own games, and spent the class coding my games. The course did teach me things I couldn't find on Wiki pages. I felt that I was actually creating something. If only there were a way to put these games on my website. That is when I learned about The Coding Train.
